2  Tests for One Continuous Population Mean

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Formulate hypotheses for testing a single population mean or proportion.

  • Choose and conduct an appropriate one-sample t-test or one-sample z-test based on study context and assumptions.

  • Differentiate between cases where the population variance is known and unknown, and select the appropriate test for each case.

  • Perform one-sample t-tests and z-tests using both R and Python, and interpret the outputs.

  • Apply statistical reasoning to real-world data examples, including evaluating assumptions and limitations.

This chapter introduces statistical tests for analyzing a single sample, a common task in data analysis across many disciplines. These methods are used when we want to compare a sample-based estimate to a known or hypothesized population value. For example, we may want to test whether the average recovery time after a treatment differs from a clinical standard, whether student test scores exceed a benchmark, or whether the proportion of successes in a group differs from an expected rate. In each case, hypothesis testing helps us assess whether the observed difference is large enough to provide evidence against the null hypothesis, or whether it could reasonably be explained by random sampling variation.

In this chapter, we review tests for making inference about a single population parameter based on one sample. We focus on two common settings:

  • A population mean, where the response variable is continuous, and
  • A population proportion, where the response variable is binary.

For a single population mean, the choice of test depends on whether the population variance is known or unknown. When the population variance is unknown, we use the one-sample t-test. When the population variance is known, we use the one-sample z-test. For binary outcomes, we use a one-sample proportion z-test to compare the population proportion to a hypothesized value.

The choice of test depends on the type of response variable, the information available about the population variance, and the sample size. Each section below introduces the test setup, hypotheses, assumptions, theoretical background, and implementation in both R and Python using real or simulated data.

2.1 One-sample t-test for the mean

2.1.1 Review

Use this test when:

  • You have one sample from a population.
  • The response variable is quantitative (also known as continuous).
  • You want to test whether the population mean is equal to a specific reference value.
  • The population variance is unknown.
  • The sample is approximately normal, or the sample size is large enough for the sampling distribution of the sample mean to be approximately normal.

In this section, we focus on the case where the population variance is unknown. The known-variance case will be discussed later in Section 2.2 using the one-sample z-test.

The one-sample t-test is used when we want to compare a sample mean to a specific reference value. Suppose we observe a random sample from a population where both the population mean \(\mu\) and population standard deviation \(\sigma\) are unknown. We use the sample mean and sample standard deviation to assess whether the population mean appears to differ from the reference value. For example, we may want to know whether the average flipper length of penguins is different from 200 mm.

The main statistical question is:

Is the population mean statistically different from the hypothesized value \(\mu_0\)?

For example, if the hypothesized value is \(\mu_0 = 200\), then the question becomes:

Does the sample provide evidence that the population mean is different from 200?

Because the population variance is unknown, we estimate it using the sample standard deviation. As we will see later in this chapter, this additional uncertainty (estimating the variance) is why the test statistic follows a t-distribution rather than a standard normal distribution.

Tip: Sample standard deviation

When the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is unknown, we estimate it using the sample standard deviation:

\[ s = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n - 1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i - \bar{X})^2}. \]

Here, \(\bar{X}\) is the sample mean, and \(s\) measures how spread out the observations are around the sample mean.

2.1.2 Study design

Now that we have reviewed the general idea behind the one-sample t-test, let us apply it to a real data example. In this section, we use the penguins data set from the palmerpenguins package in R. This data set contains measurements for penguins observed near Palmer Station, Antarctica. Some of the main quantitative variables include flipper_length_mm, which records the length of a penguin’s flipper in millimetres; bill_length_mm, which records the length of the penguin’s bill in millimetres; bill_depth_mm, which records the depth or thickness of the bill in millimetres; and body_mass_g, which records the penguin’s body mass in grams.

For this example, the response variable of interest is flipper_length_mm, which records penguin flipper length in millimetres. We treat the observed penguins in the data set as a sample from a broader population of similar penguins. Let \(\mu\) denote the population mean flipper length of penguins. Suppose we want to compare the population mean flipper length to a reference value of 200 mm. In other words, we want to assess whether the sample provides evidence that the average flipper length in the population is different from 200 mm.

The main statistical question is:

Does the data provide evidence that the population mean flipper length of penguins is different from 200 mm?

In this example, the hypothesized population mean is

\[ \mu_0 = 200. \]

To keep the exploratory and inferential parts of the analysis separate, we will split the data into a training set and a test set. The training set will be used for exploratory data analysis, and the test set will be used to conduct the formal hypothesis test.

2.1.5 Testing Settings

Before writing the hypotheses, we summarize the testing setup for this example. The response variable is flipper_length_mm, which is a quantitative variable measured in millimetres. The parameter of interest is the population mean flipper length, denoted by \(\mu\).

In this example, we compare the population mean flipper length to the reference value

\[ \mu_0 = 200. \]

Since the population standard deviation is unknown, we use the sample standard deviation to estimate it. Therefore, the appropriate test is a one-sample t-test.

The formal test will be conducted using the test set. This keeps the exploratory analysis and the hypothesis test separate: the training set was used to understand the data, while the test set is used to calculate the test statistic and p-value.

For this test, we use:

  • Response variable: flipper_length_mm
  • Parameter of interest: \(\mu\), the population mean flipper length
  • Reference value: \(\mu_0 = 200\)
  • Test: one-sample t-test
  • Population variance: unknown
  • Sample used for inference: test_set
  • Significance level: \(\alpha = 0.05\)

Because this is a one-sample t-test, we assume that the observations are independent and that the sampling distribution of the sample mean is approximately normal. The exploratory analysis suggested that the raw flipper length distribution is not perfectly normal, partly because penguin species differ in their typical flipper lengths. However, the sample size is reasonably large and there are no severe outliers, so using the one-sample t-test is still a reasonable approach for this example.

2.1.6 Hypothesis Definitions

2.1.7 Test Flavour and Components

The one-sample t-test compares the sample mean to the hypothesized population mean. In this example, the hypothesized value is \(\mu_0 = 200\) mm. Let \(\bar{x}\) denote the sample mean flipper length in the test set, and let \(s\) denote the sample standard deviation of flipper length in the test set. If the test set contains \(n\) penguins, the test statistic is

\[ t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}}. \]

In this formula:

  • \(\bar{x}\) is the sample mean flipper length.
  • \(\mu_0\) is the hypothesized population mean, which is 200 mm in this example.
  • \(s\) is the sample standard deviation.
  • \(n\) is the sample size.
  • \(s / \sqrt{n}\) is the estimated standard error of the sample mean.

The numerator, \(\bar{x} - \mu_0\), measures how far the sample mean is from the hypothesized value. The denominator, \(s / \sqrt{n}\), scales this difference by the estimated variability of the sample mean. Therefore, the test statistic measures how many estimated standard errors the sample mean is away from the hypothesized value.

Heads-up!

The test statistic \(t\) is computed from the sample. This means that \(t\) itself is a random variable: if we collected a different random sample of penguins, we would likely get a different sample mean \(\bar{x}\), a different sample standard deviation \(s\), and therefore a different value of \(t\).

Because the population standard deviation is unknown, we estimate it using the sample standard deviation \(s\). This is the key reason the null distribution is a t-distribution rather than a standard normal distribution.

Tip: Null distribution of the test statistic

Under the null hypothesis,

\[ H_0: \mu = 200, \]

the test statistic

\[ T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{S / \sqrt{n}} \]

follows a t-distribution with \(n - 1\) degrees of freedom:

\[ T \sim t_{n-1}. \]

This distribution describes the values we would expect the test statistic to take if the null hypothesis were true. Since the alternative hypothesis is two-sided, the p-value is computed as

\[ \textit{p-value} = 2 \times Pr(T_{n-1} \geq |t|), \]

where \(t\) is the test statistic computed from the observed sample.

2.1.8 Inferential Conclusions

The null distribution described above gives us a reference point for deciding whether the observed test statistic is unusually large. In other words, it tells us what values of the test statistic are typical when the null hypothesis is true.

After computing the test statistic and p-value, we compare the p-value to the significance level \(\alpha = 0.05\).

If the p-value is less than 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis. In that case, we conclude that the data provide sufficient evidence that the population mean flipper length is different from 200 mm.

If the p-value is greater than or equal to 0.05, we do not reject the null hypothesis. In that case, we conclude that the data do not provide sufficient evidence that the population mean flipper length is different from 200 mm.

It is important to phrase the second conclusion carefully. Failing to reject the null hypothesis does not prove that the population mean is exactly 200 mm. It only means that, based on the available sample, we do not have enough evidence to conclude that it differs from 200 mm.

2.2 One-sample z-test for the mean

2.2.1 Review

Use this test when:

  • You have one sample from a population.
  • The response variable is quantitative (also known as continuous).
  • You want to test whether the population mean is equal to a specific reference value.
  • The population variance, or equivalently the population standard deviation, is known.
  • The sample is approximately normal, or the sample size is large enough for the sampling distribution of the sample mean to be approximately normal.

In the previous section, we used the one-sample t-test because the population variance was unknown and had to be estimated using the sample standard deviation. In this section, we focus on a different case: the population variance is assumed to be known.

The one-sample z-test is used when we want to compare a sample mean to a hypothesized population mean and the population standard deviation is known. Suppose we observe a random sample of size \(n\),

\[ x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n, \]

from a population with unknown mean \(\mu\) but also known population standard deviation \(\sigma\). We want to assess whether the data provide enough evidence to conclude that \(\mu\) differs from a specified reference value, denoted by \(\mu_0\).

The main statistical question is:

Is the population mean statistically different from the hypothesized value \(\mu_0\)?

For example, if the hypothesized value is \(\mu_0 = 200\), then the question becomes:

Does the sample provide evidence that the population mean is different from 200?

The key difference from the one-sample t-test is how we measure uncertainty. For the t-test, the population standard deviation is unknown, so we estimate it using the sample standard deviation. For the z-test, the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is treated as known. As a result, the test statistic follows a standard normal distribution under the null hypothesis.

Quick review!

In practice, the population standard deviation is rarely known exactly. The one-sample z-test is most appropriate when \(\sigma\) is known from reliable external information, such as historical records, a very large previous study, or a well-established measurement process. When \(\sigma\) is unknown, the one-sample t-test is always the more appropriate choice.

  • \(\sigma\) is the known population standard deviation, which is assumed to be 14 mm in this example.
  • \(n\) is the sample size.
  • \(\sigma / \sqrt{n}\) is the standard error of the sample mean.

The denominator, \(\sigma / \sqrt{n}\), scales this difference by the known standard error of the sample mean. Heads-up!

The key difference between the one-sample z-test and the one-sample t-test is the standard deviation used in the denominator. \[ H_0: \mu = 200, \]

\[ Z \sim N(0, 1). \]

\[ \textit{p-value} = 2 \times Pr(Z \geq |z|), \]

def z_test_mean(x, mu=0, sigma=None, alternative="two-sided", conf_level=0.95):
    """
    One-sample z-test for a population mean when the population standard
    deviation is known.
    """

    if sigma is None:
        raise ValueError("sigma must be provided for a one-sample z-test.")

    x = np.asarray(x, dtype=float)
    x = x[~np.isnan(x)]

    n = len(x)

        z_critical = stats.norm.ppf(conf_level)
        ci_lower = -np.inf
        ci_upper = sample_mean + z_critical * standard_error

        raise ValueError("alternative must be 'two-sided', 'greater', or 'less'.")

    return pd.DataFrame({

        "sample_size": [n],
        "standard_error": [standard_error],
        "z_statistic": [z_stat],
        "p_value": [p_value],
        "ci_lower": [ci_lower],

\[ Y_i = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if observation } i \text{ has the characteristic of interest},\\ 0, & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases} \]

The population parameter of interest is \(p\), which denotes the population proportion of observations with the characteristic of interest. In this section, the characteristic of interest is having body mass greater than 4000 g. Therefore, \(p\) represents the population proportion of penguins whose body mass is greater than 4000 g.

From the sample, we estimate \(p\) using the sample proportion, denoted by \(\hat{p}\). To calculate \(\hat{p}\), we first count the number of observations that have the characteristic of interest. In our example, this is the number of penguins with body_mass_g > 4000. We then divide this count by the total sample size.

If \(x\) denotes the number of penguins with body mass greater than 4000 g, and \(n\) denotes the total number of penguins in the sample, then the sample proportion is

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{x}{n}. \]

For example, if 37 out of 70 penguins in the test set have body mass greater than 4000 g, then

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{37}{70} \approx 0.529. \]

This means that about 52.9% of penguins in the test set have body mass greater than 4000 g. We use this sample proportion, \(\hat{p}\), as an estimate of the unknown population proportion \(p\).

Now back to the main statistical question we would like to test and answer this inferential question:

Is the population proportion statistically different from the hypothesized value \(p_0\)?

In this section, we continue using the penguins data set. To create a binary response variable, we classify penguins based on whether their body mass is above 4000 grams. That is, we define a penguin as being in the group of interest if

\[ \texttt{body\_mass\_g} > 4000. \]

The corresponding binary variable is:

\[ Y_i = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if penguin } i \text{ has body mass greater than 4000 g},\\ 0, & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases} \]

For example, if the hypothesized population proportion is \(p_0 = 0.50\), then the question becomes:

Does the sample provide evidence that the population proportion of penguins with body mass above 4000 g is different from 50%?

Heads up!

The one-sample proportion z-test relies on a normal approximation to the sampling distribution of \(\hat{p}\). A common rule of thumb is that the expected number of observations in each category under the null hypothesis should be reasonably large:

\[ n p_0 \geq 10 \]

and

\[ n(1 - p_0) \geq 10. \]

When these conditions are satisfied, the z-test provides a reasonable large-sample test for the population proportion.

In this example, we create a binary variable from a quantitative variable by choosing the cutoff body_mass_g > 4000. This is useful for teaching the mechanics of a one-sample proportion test. In a real analysis, the cutoff should be chosen before conducting the formal test and should have a meaningful scientific or practical interpretation.

2.3.6 Hypothesis Definitions

We now translate the research question into formal statistical hypotheses. Recall that \(p\) denotes the population proportion of penguins with body mass greater than 4000 g, and the reference value is

\[ p_0 = 0.50. \]

Since the main statistical question asks whether the population proportion is different from 50%, we use a two-sided test.

The hypotheses are:

\[ H_0: p = 0.50 \quad \textit{vs} \quad H_A: p \neq 0.50 \]

The null hypothesis states that 50% of penguins in the population have body mass greater than 4000 g. Under this hypothesis, any difference between the observed sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) and 0.50 is attributed to random sampling variation.

The alternative hypothesis states that the population proportion of penguins with body mass greater than 4000 g is not 0.50. This includes both possibilities: the population proportion could be less than 0.50 or greater than 0.50.

In this example, a “success” means that a penguin has body mass greater than 4000 g. Therefore, the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is calculated as

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{\text{number of penguins with body mass greater than 4000 g}}{\text{total number of penguins in the sample}}. \]

For the formal test, we will use the test set and compare the observed sample proportion to the hypothesized value \(p_0 = 0.50\).

2.3.7 Test Flavour and Components

The one-sample proportion z-test compares the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) to the hypothesized population proportion \(p_0\). In this example, the hypothesized value is

\[ p_0 = 0.50. \]

Let \(x\) denote the number of penguins in the test set with body mass greater than 4000 g, and let \(n\) denote the total number of penguins in the test set. The sample proportion is

\[ \hat{p} = \frac{x}{n}. \]

For this example, a success is defined as

\[ \texttt{body\_mass\_g} > 4000. \]

The test statistic is

\[ z = \frac{\hat{p} - p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1 - p_0)/n}}. \]

In this formula:

  • \(\hat{p}\) is the sample proportion of penguins with body mass greater than 4000 g.
  • \(p_0\) is the hypothesized population proportion, which is 0.50 in this example.
  • \(n\) is the sample size.
  • \(\sqrt{p_0(1 - p_0)/n}\) is the standard error of the sample proportion under the null hypothesis.

The numerator, \(\hat{p} - p_0\), measures how far the observed sample proportion is from the hypothesized population proportion. The denominator, \(\sqrt{p_0(1 - p_0)/n}\), scales this difference by the standard error expected under the null hypothesis. Therefore, the test statistic measures how many standard errors the observed sample proportion is away from the hypothesized value.

Heads-up!

The test statistic \(z\) is computed from the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\). Since \(\hat{p}\) would change from sample to sample, the test statistic would also change from sample to sample. The null distribution tells us what values of \(z\) are typical when the true population proportion is \(p_0\).

Tip: Null distribution of the test statistic

Under the null hypothesis,

\[ H_0: p = 0.50, \]

and when the normal approximation is reasonable, the test statistic

\[ Z = \frac{\hat{P} - p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1 - p_0)/n}} \]

approximately follows a standard normal distribution:

\[ Z \approx N(0, 1). \]

The word approximately is important here. For a proportion test, the normal distribution is a large-sample approximation. This is why we check that \(np_0\) and \(n(1-p_0)\) are both reasonably large before using the test.

Since the alternative hypothesis is two-sided, the p-value is computed as

\[ \textit{p-value} = 2 \times Pr(Z \geq |z|), \]

where \(z\) is the test statistic computed from the observed sample.

2.3.8 Inferential Conclusions

The goal of the one-sample proportion z-test is to decide whether the observed sample proportion is far enough from the hypothesized value to provide evidence against the null hypothesis.

The null distribution described above gives us a reference point for deciding whether the observed value of \(z\) is unusually large. After computing the test statistic and p-value, we compare the p-value to the significance level \(\alpha = 0.05\).

If the p-value is less than 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis. In that case, we conclude that the data provide sufficient evidence that the population proportion of penguins with body mass greater than 4000 g is different from 50%.

If the p-value is greater than or equal to 0.05, we do not reject the null hypothesis. In that case, we conclude that the data do not provide sufficient evidence that the population proportion of penguins with body mass greater than 4000 g is different from 50%.

As before, failing to reject the null hypothesis does not prove that the population proportion is exactly 0.50. It only means that, based on the available sample, we do not have enough evidence to conclude that it differs from 0.50.
